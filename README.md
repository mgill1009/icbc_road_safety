## Overview

This project analyzes **1.37 million crash records** from the Insurance Corporation of British Columbia (ICBC) to investigate structural inequalities in road safety outcomes across BC from 2020 to 2024.

**Core thesis:** Vulnerable road users — pedestrians, cyclists, and motorcyclists — face disproportionately higher injury risk than car occupants, and this gap is widening. The analysis demonstrates that this inequality is structural and infrastructure-driven rather than explained by temporal or behavioural factors.

## Key Findings

### Vulnerable User Risk
- Pedestrians face an **80.5% casualty rate** — 4 in 5 crashes result in injury
- Cyclists: **63.7%** | Motorcyclists: **54.6%** | Car Occupants: **16.6%**
- After controlling for all other factors, a pedestrian crash has **28× higher odds** of resulting in injury than a car occupant crash (logistic regression, OR=28.06, 95% CI [19.82–39.72])

### The Gap is Widening
- Relative Index of Inequality (RII) for pedestrians rose from **4.20× (2020)** to **5.44× (2024)** — a 29% increase in relative inequality in 4 years
- Cyclist RII increased from **3.29×** to **4.42×** (+34%)
- Car occupant casualty rates declined from 19% → 15% over the same period

### Intersections
- Intersection crashes carry a **7× higher casualty odds** than non-intersection crashes (OR=7.17, tightest CI in the model)
- 41% of all BC crashes occur at intersections
- Cyclist intersection crashes are 26 percentage points more deadly than mid-block crashes

### Temporal Patterns
- Season, hour of day, and weekend/weekday are **not independently predictive** once road user type, intersection, and region are controlled for — the EDA patterns are driven by *who* is on the road, not the time itself
- Late-night weekend crashes (Sat/Sun 01:00) have the highest casualty rates in the dataset (~23%)

### Geographic Variation
- Metro Vancouver dominates crash *volume* (57% of all crashes)
- Smaller municipalities (Spallumcheen 30.6%, View Royal 27.7%) have the highest casualty *rates*
- Vancouver's vulnerable user problem is primarily cyclists (~45%); Surrey's is pedestrians (~60%)

## Data Source

**ICBC Open Data** — publicly available crash records published by the Insurance Corporation of British Columbia.

- **URL:** https://www.icbc.com/about-icbc/newsroom/Statistics
- **Coverage:** 2020–2024 (5 years, ~1.37M records)
- **Encoding:** UTF-16, tab-separated
- **Note:** Analysis limited to 2020–2024 as ICBC's structured open data format was introduced in 2020. Pre-2020 records follow different reporting conventions shaped by legislative changes, making direct comparison unreliable. The 2020 baseline is suppressed by COVID-19 lockdowns and should be interpreted with caution.

### Data Limitations
- No coordinates or precise addresses — municipality level only
- No demographic data (age, gender) in ICBC crash records
- No contributing factor data (speed, impairment, distraction)
- Binary severity only: Casualty Crash vs Property Damage Only (PDO) — no fatality flag

### 03 — Modelling

Two complementary modelling approaches:

#### Logistic Regression (Inference)
**Purpose:** Quantify the independent effect of each feature on casualty risk after controlling for all other variables.

**Why statsmodels (not sklearn):** Produces odds ratios, p-values, and confidence intervals — essential for inference. sklearn is optimised for prediction and does not return these.

**Key decisions:**
- 15,000 stratified sample (50/50 class balance) — prevents quasi-complete separation that occurs on the full 1.37M dataset
- Reference categories: Car Occupant (road user), Metro Vancouver (region), Summer (season)
- `crash_config` excluded — causes separation even at 15k rows
- Optimizer: `bfgs`, `maxiter=500`, converged in 163 iterations

**Top results:**

| Feature | Odds Ratio | 95% CI | p-value |
|---------|-----------|--------|---------|
| Pedestrian (vs Car Occupant) | **28.06** | [19.82 – 39.72] | < 0.001 |
| Cyclist (vs Car Occupant) | **9.54** | [6.95 – 13.08] | < 0.001 |
| Intersection | **7.17** | [6.79 – 7.58] | < 0.001 |
| Motorcyclist (vs Car Occupant) | **7.17** | [5.44 – 9.45] | < 0.001 |
| Fraser Valley (vs Metro Van) | **1.34** | [1.20 – 1.49] | < 0.001 |
| Year (per year) | **0.929** | [0.912 – 0.947] | < 0.001 |
| Heavy Vehicle (vs Car Occupant) | **0.661** | [0.583 – 0.750] | < 0.001 |
| Season, Hour, Weekend | ~1.0 | — | > 0.05 (not significant) |

#### XGBoost (Prediction)
**Purpose:** Predict casualty vs PDO crashes with maximum accuracy. Captures non-linear relationships.

**Configuration:**
```python
XGBClassifier(
    n_estimators=300,       # converges at ~150
    max_depth=5,
    learning_rate=0.1,
    scale_pos_weight=4.6,   # handles 82/18 class imbalance
    enable_categorical=True,
    tree_method="hist"
)
```

**Top features by Gain (most meaningful importance measure):**
1. `intersection`
2. `road_user_type`
3. `crash_config`
4. `region`
5. `hour_approx`

| Model | AUC-ROC | F1 (Casualty) | F1 (Weighted) | Primary Use |
|-------|---------|--------------|---------------|-------------|
| Logistic Regression | 0.761 | 0.754 | 0.738 | Inference (why) |

## Methodology Notes

### Class Imbalance
The target variable is heavily imbalanced: **82.2% PDO vs 17.8% Casualty**. Raw accuracy is not a meaningful metric — a model predicting PDO for every crash would score 82% accuracy with 0% casualty recall. We report **AUC-ROC** and **F1 on the casualty class** as primary metrics.

### Inference vs Prediction
- **Inference (Logistic Regression):** Answers *why* — which features independently increase casualty risk, and by how much, after controlling for everything else. Produces odds ratios with confidence intervals.
- **Prediction (XGBoost):** Answers *what* — given these features, can we predict whether a crash will result in a casualty? Produces a probability score.

### Region Grouping
Municipalities are grouped into 5 regions for modelling:

| Region | Key Municipalities |
|--------|-------------------|
| Metro Vancouver | Vancouver, Surrey, Burnaby, Richmond, Coquitlam, Delta, Langley... |
| Fraser Valley | Abbotsford, Chilliwack, Mission, Hope... |
| Vancouver Island | Victoria, Saanich, Nanaimo, Campbell River... |
| Interior | Kelowna, Kamloops, Prince George, Vernon... |
| Northern/Other | All remaining municipalities |

## Limitations

- **No contributing factors:** ICBC data does not capture speed, impairment, distraction, or road geometry — the most direct determinants of crash severity. Adding these would likely push model AUC above 0.85.
- **No coordinates:** Geographic analysis is limited to municipality level. Street-level hotspot analysis is possible via the `road_location` column but labels are verbose intersection descriptions.
- **Binary severity only:** The dataset distinguishes casualty crashes from PDO but does not indicate fatality severity within casualty crashes.
- **2020 COVID effect:** Traffic volumes were significantly reduced in 2020, suppressing crash rates. This makes 2020 a problematic baseline for trend analysis.
- **Observational data:** Associations identified in this analysis cannot be interpreted as causal without a controlled study design.
- **Logistic regression sample:** Coefficients estimated on 15k stratified sample — sufficient for stable inference but not the full population.
